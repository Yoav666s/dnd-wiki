Cortenmaas is a vast and unforgiving continent, its identity shaped by endless deserts that stretch beyond the horizon and the scattered oases that sustain life within them. These oases are more than mere sources of water--they are centers of culture, trade, and power, often fiercely contested and deeply revered. Ancient caravan routes weave between them, forming the arteries of civilization, while the open sands in between remain perilous and largely unclaimed, home to shifting ruins, nomadic peoples, and things long buried beneath the dunes.

# Briss
Briss did not begin as a nation so much as a stubborn refusal to accept the limits of the desert. Where other peoples clung to what water they were given, the early circles of Briss chose to make water instead. At first, their work was modest—small gatherings of druids walking the edges of an oasis, calling brief rains from clear skies through repeated invocations of Create or Destroy Water. The spell alone could not transform the land, but persistence did what power could not. Day after day, season after season, they layered rainfall upon rainfall, allowing the soil to soften, then hold, then remember.

Over generations, this practice became ritualized. Entire communities were built around coordinated castings, timed with planting cycles and guided by careful stewardship of the land. Druids taught not only magic but restraint, ensuring that each expansion of fertile ground was stabilized with native grasses, deep-rooted trees, and carefully managed irrigation channels. What began as isolated oases grew outward in uneven halos of green, eventually linking with neighboring sites. Caravan routes shifted to follow these widening belts of life, and trade flourished in their shade.

The Briss of today is unlike any other land in Cortenmaas. From afar, its borders appear as a mirage that does not fade—a patchwork of orchards, terraced fields, and shallow lakes stitched into the desert. Rainfall, though still unnatural, is no longer sporadic; it comes in measured intervals, summoned by rotating druidic orders who maintain an unbroken lineage of knowledge. The air itself feels different here, heavier with moisture, carrying the scent of soil and growth rather than dust.
